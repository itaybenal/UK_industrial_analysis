# UK Productivity and the "Informality Penalty" 
## Project Summary
This project analyses the 'Informality Penalty' in the UK economy (2015–2023).   
By correlating regional economic output (GVA) with workforce structures across 20 industries,   
I identified a structural divide where 50% of Britian's productivity is generated by industries highly sensitive to informal labour,   
specifically in 'Scale' sectors such as Retail and Accommodation.

*Raw counts were converted into percentages and normalised.*

#### Industry Classification
*Industry Type is determined by the Pearson Correlation (r) between an industry's GVA per FTE and its 4 business sizes.*
| Industry Type | Formula | Description |
| ---------  | ---------   | -----------      |
| Scale | r(gva_per_fte, Large) > 0.3 | Productivity is driven by Large businesses. |
| Specialisation | r(gva_per_fte, Micro) > 0.3 | Productivity is driven by Micro businesses. |
| Dual | r(gva_per_fte, Micro) > 0.3 **AND** r(gva_per_fte, Large) > 0.2 | Industry shows dual correlation. |
| Hybrid | No thresholds met | Productivity is not strictly tied to Large or Micro businesses. |

## Methodology

### Custom Metric Creation:
Two custom metrics were created:  
1. **Productivity (GVA per FTE):**  
Calculated Full-Time Equivalents (FTE) to normalise labour counts:  
FTE = Full-Time Workers + (Part-Time Workers × 0.5)  
GVA per FTE = Total Regional GVA / FTE  
​
2. **Worker Informality Ratio:**  
This metric identifies the gap between Full-Time Equivalents and total worker headcount (which includes working owners and self-employed).  
Informality Ratio = Total Worker Headcount − FTE / Total Worker Headcount​  
High ratios signify an industry's heavy reliance on self-employment and part-time workers.  

### Industry Classification
A central pillar of this project was the classification of 20 industries,  
based on their relationship between Productivity and the prevelance of various business sizes. 

**Using the Pearson correlation (r), industries were mapped into four quadrants:**  
	 
* <ins>Scale</ins>: Productivity shows a positive correlation with percentage of Large businesses. (Scale-driven).  
* <ins>Specialisation</ins>: Productivity shows a positive correlation with percentage of Micro businesses. (Specialisation-driven).  
* <ins>Hybrid</ins>: Balanced correlation across Micro and Large business sizes.  
* <ins>Dual</ins>: Specific to the 'Health' industry, which shows correlation with both Micro and Large businesses.  

A Pearson Correlation threshold of >= 0.3 was selected to define industry types.   
This threshold was chosen to capture meaningful trends while filtering out weak correlation.  

### The Analysis Pipeline
1. <ins>Normalisation:</ins> Business size counts were converted to percentages and normalised to allow for comparison between industries.  
2. <ins>Industry Classification:</ins> Classified industries by 'Scale', 'Specialisation', 'Hybrid' or 'Dual'.  
3. <ins>Informality Correlation:</ins> Calculated the relationship between GVA per FTE and the Informality Ratio for each industry.  
4. <ins>Joining Insights:</ins> Joined Industry Classification (Scale vs. Specialisation) with Informality Correlation to test for the "Informality Penalty" - which industries were penalised by informal workforces and which were resilient.

## Insights

### 1. Businesses Formalise as they Grow
Growth acts as a primary driver for business formalisation. As businesses scale into Medium and Large categories,   
the relationship between size and informality grows ever more negative in correlation (r= -0.30 for Large ),   
showing that labour formality is linked with business scaling.

### 2. The Structural Divide: 'Scale' vs. 'Specialisation'
The impact of informality is not equal - it is dictated by an industry’s "structural DNA":
* Scale-Driven Industries (Finance, Retail): Informality acts as a drag on productivity, with an average productivity penalty 10x higher than in other sectors. 55% of these industries show a strong negative link (r <-0.5) between informal labour and productivity.
* Specialisation-Led Industries (Construction, Property): Productivity is often maintained regardless of labour formality, showing a near-zero average correlation.

### 3. Financial Impact "Red Zone"
The "Red Zone" represents industries where informality is a direct, moderate-to-strong threat to productivity (r <- 0.3).
* 50% of the UK’s total GVA is generated by Scale-driven industries sensitive to this "informality penalty."
* Nearly half of all UK industrial output (as of 2023) originates within this "Red Zone",  
  making worker informality a systemic obstacle in scale-driven industries that underpin Britain's economy.

## Limitations

**Correlation does not equal causality** -  
This project is based on correlation analysis.   
It does not prove that informality causes low productivity.  
Further research is needed to determine if informality causes low productivity or if low-productivity firms simply use informal labour to stay afloat.  

**Public vs. Private data** -  
GVA data (divided by SIC 2007 industry codes) includes both public and private GVA, whereas informality metrics focus on private sector data exclusively.

**Exclusion of 'Public Administration'** -  
The industry 'Public Administration and Defence' has been excluded from analysis as it only contains data from the public sector.

**SIC code combinations** -  
The final list of industries (21) is built from combining 'Broad industrial groups' (highest categorisation) and 'Sections' (2nd highest) into a single, detailed list of general industries.

**Self-employed data** -  
Employment data (BRES) includes self-employed individuals only if registered for VAT/PAYE. Unregistered self-employed are excluded.

**No Northern Ireland data** -  
Following complications in the data extraction phase,   
NI data has been excluded from analysis.

**Null Handling** -  
Records with missing values for key metrics were excluded from correlation calculations. This affects a small minority of observations.
